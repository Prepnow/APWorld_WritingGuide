SAQ

Short Answer

3 parts (a, b, c). Each part is 2–4 sentences. No thesis. No intro. Just answer directly, with evidence. Your enemy is over-writing.

DBQ

Document-Based Question

Analyze 7 documents to write a historical argument. Uses outside knowledge + document analysis. Your enemy is only summarizing documents.

LEQ

Long Essay Question

Write a full essay from memory. Choose one of three prompts. No documents provided. Your enemy is overthinking and a blank first paragraph.

The Root Problem

Why Students Overthink the LAQ

The LAQ feels overwhelming because it has no guardrails — no documents, no excerpt, just a blank page and a prompt. Most students treat it like an English essay (perfect thesis, ideal evidence, polished prose) when the AP rubric rewards something much more specific and formulaic.

The fix: Replace "write a great essay" with "hit 6 specific checkboxes in 40 minutes." The rubric is mechanical. Your response should be too. Once you internalize the rubric, the overthinking disappears because there's nothing to figure out — you just execute the checklist.

Section 2

SAQ — Short Answer Mastery

The SAQ rewards students who can be precise and concise. Most lost points come from vague answers or skipping the "why." Here's the formula.

The ACE Formula — One Method for Every SAQ Part

Every SAQ part, no matter how it's phrased, can be answered with exactly three moves:

A
Answer the question
One sentence. Make a direct, specific claim that responds to exactly what was asked.
C
Cite specific evidence
One sentence. Name a specific example — event, person, policy, or development that supports your claim.
E
Explain the connection
One sentence. Say HOW or WHY your evidence proves your claim. This is the sentence most students skip.
Timing

The 13-Minute Rule

You have 40 minutes for 3 SAQs = ~13 minutes each. That's about 4 minutes per part (a, b, c). Don't let one part balloon — a 3-sentence answer is the ceiling, not the floor.

If you can't answer a SAQ part in 3 sentences, you're adding details the rubric doesn't need. Stop, move on, come back.
Common Mistakes

What Loses Points

  • Vague claims: "Trade was important" → no point
  • Skipping the E: Evidence without "because/therefore/this led to" = incomplete
  • Writing a paragraph intro: No thesis needed, no background needed
  • Not answering the exact verb: "Describe" ≠ "Explain" ≠ "Evaluate"
  • Over-writing: 6 sentences when 3 earn full credit

SAQ Verb Decoder — What Each Command Means

VerbWhat it wantsTemplate starter
DescribeState what happened. No analysis needed — just accurate, specific facts."[Topic] was characterized by... For example, [specific event/example]..."
ExplainState what happened AND why, or how it connects to a cause/effect."[Topic] occurred because... This is demonstrated by [evidence], which shows..."
EvaluateMake a judgment — argue whether something was significant, effective, or true."[Topic] was [most/least] significant because... [Evidence] demonstrates that..."
AnalyzeBreak something down into its parts, causes, or effects."[Topic] can be understood through [cause/factor]. [Evidence] illustrates..."
ContrastIdentify a meaningful difference between two things."Unlike [X], [Y] was characterized by... This difference is evident in [evidence]."

Annotated Example SAQ — See ACE in Action

Prompt: "Explain ONE way the Silk Roads contributed to cultural change in Eurasia between 200 BCE and 1400 CE."
Sample Response (full credit)
The Silk Roads contributed to the spread of Buddhism from South Asia into Central and East Asia. Buddhist missionaries and merchants traveling the Silk Roads brought Buddhist teachings, texts, and artistic traditions to China, Korea, and Japan, evidenced by the construction of thousands of Buddhist cave temples along the routes, such as those at Dunhuang, China. This exchange transformed the religious and cultural landscape of East Asia, where Buddhism became a major faith that shaped governance, art, and social life for centuries.
Yellow = Answer (direct claim) Green = Cite (specific evidence) Blue = Explain (the connection)

SAQ Part C — The Comparison or Evaluation Part

SAQ Part C almost always asks you to compare, evaluate, or place something in a broader context. It's slightly harder but follows the same ACE structure.

Part C Prompt Type 1

"Explain ONE similarity or difference between..."

  • A = "One similarity/difference between X and Y is..."
  • C = Name the specific shared or contrasting feature with examples from both
  • E = "This is significant because both/whereas..."
Part C Prompt Type 2

"Evaluate the extent to which..."

  • A = Take a position — "To a significant/limited extent..."
  • C = Give your strongest piece of evidence for that position
  • E = Acknowledge a counter-consideration in one clause ("although...")
Section 3

DBQ — Document-Based Question

The DBQ is the most complex question on the exam, but also the most formulaic. 7 points, 7 documents, 60 minutes. Each point has a specific mechanical requirement.

Rubric — All 7 Points

Exactly What You're Scoring For

1
Thesis/Claim — A historically defensible claim that responds to the prompt. Must be in the intro OR conclusion. One sentence is enough — no need for a long intro.
1
Contextualization — Describe a historical context that is BROADER than the prompt and connects to it. Must be a full developed paragraph (3+ sentences), NOT just a phrase. This is the hardest point to earn — write it first.
1
Evidence: Content — Use content from at least 3 documents to address the topic. Just accurately describe what the documents say — no analysis needed for this point alone.
2
Evidence: Sourcing (HAPP) — Explain how the Historical context, Audience, Purpose, or Point of View of at least 3 documents is relevant to the argument. (Up to 2 points — one per additional sourcing beyond the first.) "Because the author was [X], the document [does/does not reflect Y] because..."
1
Analysis & Reasoning: Complexity — Demonstrate a complex understanding. Easiest way: acknowledge a corroboration (two documents that agree and explain why), a qualification (a document that complicates your argument), or connect to a different time period.
1
Outside Evidence (Beyond the Docs) — Add at least one specific piece of evidence NOT in the documents. Name a specific event, person, treaty, or development that supports your argument.

The 60-Minute DBQ Blueprint — Minute by Minute

Overthinking the DBQ usually means spending too long reading and not enough time writing. This plan fixes that.

0–3
Read the prompt — 3 minutes

Identify: What historical period? What skill is being tested (causation, CCOT, comparison)? Write the time period and skill at the top of your scratch paper.

3–18
Annotate documents — 15 minutes

Read each document once. For each one, write in the margin: (1) the main point in 5 words, (2) one HAPP note (who wrote this and why does that matter?). Group docs by perspective or theme as you go.

Resist the urge to write full analysis during the read. Just gather ammo. Annotation is preparation, not writing.
18–22
Plan your argument — 4 minutes

On scratch paper: write your thesis in one sentence. List 2–3 body paragraph themes and which documents support each. Identify your outside evidence. Identify your complexity move.

22–27
Write contextualization paragraph — 5 minutes

Write this first because it's the hardest point and requires the most time. 3–4 sentences: describe the broader historical context (what was happening in the world BEFORE or AROUND this topic), then explicitly connect it to the prompt.

27–28
Write thesis — 1 minute

One sentence. Append it to the intro paragraph. Must make a specific, defensible claim and preview your argument. Don't start with "Throughout history..."

28–55
Write body paragraphs — 27 minutes

2–3 body paragraphs. Each paragraph: topic sentence → use 2–3 documents with HAPP sourcing → outside evidence in at least one paragraph → complexity move in one paragraph.

55–60
Conclusion — 5 minutes

Restate your thesis in different words. Add a complexity sentence if you haven't already. If you're running out of time, skip the conclusion — body points are worth more.

Most Missed Point

Contextualization — The #1 Lost Point

Contextualization requires a developed paragraph of historical context that is broader than the prompt and explicitly connected to it. Students fail by writing one sentence or by describing the documents instead of the broader world.

Template: "In the [period] before/during [topic], [broader trend/event] was transforming [region]. [2 more sentences of detail.] This context shaped [the topic in the prompt] because..."
HAPP Sourcing

How to Write a Sourcing Sentence

Sourcing (POV, audience, purpose, historical context) must be tied to the argument — not just identified. The key sentence structure is:

"Because this document was written by [who], who had [relationship to topic], it likely [emphasizes/downplays/reflects] [specific aspect], which [supports/complicates] my argument that [your claim]."
DBQ Thesis — What Earns the Point vs. What Doesn't

Earns the point: A historically defensible claim that responds to the prompt with a line of reasoning. Must be more than just restating the prompt.

✓ "Although the Mongol conquests initially disrupted Eurasian trade routes, the Pax Mongolica that followed ultimately expanded and intensified trade by providing security and political stability across Central Asia."

Does NOT earn the point:

  • "Throughout history, trade has been important to many civilizations." (Too vague)
  • "The Mongols affected trade." (No argument or line of reasoning)
  • "This essay will discuss how the Mongols changed trade." (Announcement, not a claim)
  • A thesis placed in the middle of a body paragraph (must be in intro or conclusion)
Outside Evidence — How to Use It Strategically

Outside evidence means information NOT contained in any of the 7 documents. It earns 1 point and must be specific — not vague general knowledge.

How to identify your outside evidence during planning: After reading the documents, ask yourself: "What important development related to this topic is NOT represented in the docs?" Name a specific treaty, person, event, or statistic.

How to integrate it: Don't create a separate paragraph for it. Drop it into a body paragraph naturally: "...Furthermore, evidence beyond the documents corroborates this point — [specific outside example] demonstrates [connection to argument]."

Complexity — The Easiest Way to Earn It

Complexity is worth 1 point and many students skip it because it sounds intimidating. But it can be earned in several simple ways:

  • Corroboration: Show how two documents from different POVs agree on something and explain why that matters.
  • Qualification: "While most documents suggest X, Document [Y] complicates this by showing Z, which reveals..."
  • Different time period: "This pattern continued/changed after the period because [later event]."
  • Different scale: Connect the prompt's topic to a global or local pattern beyond what the docs cover.

One sentence strategically placed in a body paragraph is enough.

Section 4

LEQ — Long Essay Question

No documents. No excerpts. Just you, the prompt, and 40 minutes. The LEQ feels the hardest because it has no guardrails — so we build our own.

Rubric — All 6 Points

The LEQ Scoring Breakdown

1
Thesis/Claim — A historically defensible claim with a line of reasoning (not just a restatement of the prompt). In intro or conclusion.
1
Contextualization — Same as DBQ: a developed paragraph connecting broader historical context to the argument. 3+ sentences. Must be explicitly linked.
2
Evidence: Specific Examples — Two points available. Earn 1 pt for using at least one specific piece of evidence. Earn a second pt for using evidence across at least TWO different time periods or regions relevant to the prompt (for CCOT/comparison prompts).
2
Analysis & Reasoning — Demonstrate the required skill (causation, CCOT, or comparison) with specific evidence. Earn full 2 pts by demonstrating complexity — nuanced causation, significant continuity AND change, or a meaningful comparison with explanation.

The 40-Minute LEQ Blueprint — Cure for Overthinking

The root of LEQ overthinking is spending too long on the blank page before writing. This plan eliminates that by front-loading structure.

0–2
Read all 3 prompts, pick yours — 2 minutes

Pick the prompt where you can name the most specific examples — not the "most interesting" one. Specificity beats familiarity every time. Circle it and don't second-guess.

2–8
Brain-dump on scratch paper — 6 minutes

Write everything you know about the topic: events, people, dates, causes, effects, comparisons. Don't organize yet. Just dump. Set a hard stop at 6 minutes no matter what.

Most overthinking happens here when students try to organize while brain-dumping. Keep them separate. Dump first, structure second.
8–12
Build your outline — 4 minutes

On scratch paper: (1) write thesis in one sentence, (2) pick 2 body paragraph themes with 2–3 evidence examples each, (3) identify your context paragraph topic. That's your whole plan.

12–17
Write contextualization paragraph — 5 minutes

Same rule as DBQ: broader than the prompt, 3+ sentences, explicitly connected to your argument at the end.

17–18
Write thesis — 1 minute

One or two sentences max. Defensible claim + line of reasoning. If your thesis takes more than 2 minutes, it's too complicated — simplify it.

18–36
Write body paragraphs — 18 minutes

2 body paragraphs, ~9 min each. Topic sentence → 2–3 specific pieces of evidence → analysis connecting evidence to your thesis → complexity sentence in at least one paragraph.

36–40
Conclusion — 4 minutes

Restate thesis in different words. One sentence connecting to a broader pattern. If you're out of time, skip it — the intro + body paragraphs contain all 6 rubric points anyway.

LEQ by Skill Type — What Each Prompt Actually Wants

Causation

Cause & Effect

Identify 2–3 causes OR effects. Rank them if asked. Explain how each one led to the outcome.

Thesis pattern: "[Topic] was primarily caused by [X] and [Y], though [Z] also contributed, because..."

CCOT

Change Over Time

Identify what changed AND what stayed the same. Periodize — explain WHEN the change happened and what caused the turning point.

Thesis pattern: "While [continuity] persisted throughout, [change] occurred due to [cause], fundamentally transforming..."

Comparison

Comparison

Identify meaningful similarities AND differences between two things. Don't just list — explain WHY they differed or were alike.

Thesis pattern: "Although [X] and [Y] shared [similarity], they differed significantly in [aspect] because..."

The Overthinking Fix

The "Good Enough Thesis" Rule

Most students spend 10–15 minutes trying to write a perfect thesis. The rubric awards the thesis point for being historically defensible and having a line of reasoning — that's it. It does not need to be elegant, balanced, or comprehensive.

The test: Does your thesis make a specific claim? Does it say WHY or HOW? If yes → it earns the point. Move on. Spending more than 2 minutes on your thesis is a waste of time that costs you body paragraph points.

LEQ Thesis Examples — Good vs. Not Yet

✓ Earns the Point
  • "The spread of Islam across Afro-Eurasia between 600–1450 was driven primarily by trade networks and political conquest, which together enabled rapid cultural and commercial exchange across previously disconnected regions."
  • "While European imperialism transformed the political systems of colonized regions, it paradoxically reinforced traditional social hierarchies by relying on local elites to administer colonial rule."
✗ Does Not Earn
  • "Throughout history, many civilizations have been affected by trade." (No claim, no line of reasoning)
  • "This essay will analyze the causes of the Industrial Revolution." (Announcement, not an argument)
  • "The Industrial Revolution had both positive and negative effects." (Too vague — every historical event does)

The Sunk Cost Trap

Stop Editing, Keep Moving

The single biggest time waster on the LAQ is editing sentences you've already written. Once a sentence is on the page, leave it. You get zero extra points for polishing prose — you get points for hitting rubric checkboxes.

Rule: Never re-read a sentence you just wrote. Look only at your outline to decide what to write next.
If You Get Stuck

The 30-Second Unstuck Rule

If you stare at a blank sentence for more than 30 seconds, use the emergency move:

  1. Write the weakest version of the claim you can think of
  2. Follow it with the most obvious piece of evidence you know
  3. Add "this shows that [restate your thesis claim]"
A simple, complete argument scores more than a sophisticated, unfinished one.

The Two-Column Method — Your Exam Day Planning Sheet

Before writing a single word of your DBQ or LEQ, draw a quick two-column scratch paper plan. This takes 4–5 minutes and saves 15+ minutes of dead time.

If You're Running Out of Time

Emergency Triage Protocol

  • 10 min left: Finish your current body paragraph. Skip the conclusion — the intro + body paragraphs contain all rubric points.
  • 5 min left: Write bullet points instead of full paragraphs. List evidence with "because" phrases. This earns partial evidence points.
  • 2 min left: Write a conclusion that restates your thesis. Even one sentence re-earning the thesis point in the conclusion can save you if your intro thesis was weak.
  • Never leave a question blank. A partial SAQ earns 1–2 points. An empty question earns 0.

Week-by-Week Study Plan

Focused on building the two things that matter most: speed and structural automaticity — so you don't have to think about format on exam day.

Week
1

Learn the Frameworks Cold

  • Read the SAQ ACE framework until you can write it from memory
  • Read the DBQ and LEQ rubrics out loud — all 7 and 6 points
  • Write the thesis templates (causation, CCOT, comparison) on index cards
  • Practice writing 3 SAQ responses per day using ACE — 13 min each, timed
  • Goal: ACE is automatic; no thinking needed to structure an answer
Week
2

DBQ Document Annotation Practice

  • Do one full DBQ document set per day (just annotation — no full essay yet)
  • For each doc: write the main point in 5 words, one HAPP note, and which "side" it represents
  • Practice writing sourcing sentences: "Because this was written by X, who had Y relationship to the topic, it likely [reflects/downplays] Z because..."
  • Day 7: Write one full timed DBQ (60 min). Don't worry about quality — focus on finishing
Week
3

LEQ Speed Drills — Kill the Overthinking

  • Each day: set a timer for 8 min and write a full LEQ plan (thesis + outline) for a new prompt. No writing yet — just the plan
  • Compare your plans to the rubric: does your thesis earn the point? Do you have 2+ specific pieces of evidence per paragraph?
  • Day 4: Write one full timed LEQ (40 min). Strict time. If you're not writing by minute 12, you're overplanning
  • Day 5–6: Write one timed SAQ set AND one timed LEQ back to back to simulate exam fatigue
Week
4

Full Simulation + Rubric Self-Scoring

  • Day 1–2: Simulate the entire writing section (SAQ + DBQ + LEQ) in one sitting
  • Day 3: Score yourself against the official rubric. Circle every point you earned and every point you missed
  • Day 4: Identify your weakest point (contextualization? Complexity? Running out of time?) and drill ONLY that for 2 days
  • Day 6: One final light practice — write just a thesis and outline for 3 random prompts. Stay sharp, don't exhaust yourself
  • Day 7: Rest
Exam
Week

Final Prep

  • Review the Quick Reference section once per day — both the SAQ ACE formula and the rubric checklists
  • Re-read your best timed practice essay to remember what good execution feels like
  • Night before: do NOT write. Read the rubric checklists one time, then stop
  • Morning of: eat breakfast, bring extra pens. When you open the LEQ — pick the prompt with the most specific evidence you can recall, not the "best" topic
